The Felt Image

An exploration of how visual constructs translate into sensory space.

This project explores how 2D visual constructs can translate into sensory spatial conditions.

Built as a click-to-reveal experience, it layers texture, light, and sound to question modernist “clarity.”

Sabrina Ellison Advisor: Jackilin Bloom M.Arch 2
IG: @bina.elli • Portfolio

Thesis

This thesis explores how two-dimensional visual constructs can act as sensory translators that shift and skew spatial perception. It asks how public and semi-public environments might be reimagined through layered, material-driven interventions that rethink how space is seen, touched, and understood. The project uses a neutral warehouse as its container, not as an architectural type to be celebrated, but as a blank shell to be reoccupied with conflicting sensory logics.

Process

The design process begins with the author’s personal perception of space as someone with synesthesia, an experience where senses overlap and blend. Early work involved creating dissolving, layered collages to externalize this perceptual multiplicity. These collages were then digitally transformed using Midjourney AI—not to produce architectural renderings, but to investigate how different material atmospheres could alter the same structural container. Through iterative blending, each image proposed a distinct material effect (porous, reflective, fuzzy, translucent) with its own sensory implications.

Translation

From these blended images, the project isolates and translates specific material effects into vignettes. Each vignette is a prototype that tests the tension between what is seen and what is felt, between surface and depth, between clarity and confusion. The work draws from Mies van der Rohe’s collage techniques, using them not to assert perspectival control but to introduce friction and misalignment. It also references immersive art and architecture practices—Olafur Eliasson, James Turrell, Yayoi Kusama, and Philip Beesley—engaging the body through atmosphere, light, repetition, and interaction.

Proposal

The final model reoccupies the warehouse shell with overlapping, conflicting material systems. Rather than a single, unified reading, it proposes architecture as a layered, unstable field—one that resists resolution and invites multiple modes of sensing. The Felt Image asks how architecture can be designed not only to be seen, but to be felt.
